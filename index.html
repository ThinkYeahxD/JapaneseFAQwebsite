Japanese Culture FAQ

Momiji refers to the Japanese maple and its brilliant red leaves during autumn. It's one of the most beloved seasonal spectacles in Japan, celebrated much like cherry blossoms in spring.

Japan is made up of four main islands which are Hokkaido, Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu. Honshu is the largest and most populated island where major cities like Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, and Hiroshima are found. Japan also has more than 14,000 smaller islands. Because Japan is located in the Pacific Ring of Fire, it has many volcanoes and experiences earthquakes. The country also has four seasons: spring, summer, fall, and winter.

The current emperor of Japan is Emperor Naruhito. He is part of the Japanese Imperial Family, which is the oldest hereditary monarchy in the world. The emperor today does not have political power but serves as a symbol of unity and culture for the Japanese people. The imperial crest of Japan is a 16-petaled chrysanthemum flower, and it represents the imperial family.

A Shogun was a military leader who held real power in Japan, while the emperor was mainly a spiritual figure. The Samurai were loyal warriors who served the Shogun and followed the Bushidō code, which means “way of the warrior.” Bushidō teaches loyalty, courage, honor, and discipline. Samurai were respected members of society and represented bravery and order during Japan's feudal times.

Ninjas, also called Shinobi, were secret agents who worked for powerful lords during Japan's wars. They were skilled in spying, disguise, stealth, and self-defense. Unlike samurai who fought with honor, ninjas used strategy and surprise to win. They came from farmers and villagers who learned to protect themselves, and later became important in gathering information and doing secret missions.

Bowing, called Ojigi, is a big part of Japanese manners. It shows respect, gratitude, apology, or greeting. There are many types of bows:

Eshaku - a light bow (about 15°) used for casual greetings.

Keirei - a formal bow (30°-45°) used for elders or in business.

Saikerei - the deepest bow, showing great respect, used in ceremonies or to royalty.

Dogeza - bowing while kneeling and touching the floor, used for strong apology or deep respect.
The deeper and longer the bow, the more respect you show.

Before eating, Japanese people say “Itadakimasu”, which means “I humbly receive.” After eating, they say “Gochisousama deshita”, which means “Thank you for the meal.”
Here are some dining rules in Japan:

Slurping noodles is polite because it shows you enjoy the food.

Do not stick chopsticks upright in rice, because it looks like a funeral offering.

Do not pass food from chopstick to chopstick, also related to funeral rituals.

Use chopstick rests to keep them clean.

Don't point or play with chopsticks.
These rules show respect and cleanliness while eating.

Cleanliness is very important in Japanese life. People remove their shoes before entering houses to keep floors clean. Streets are clean even without many trash bins, because people bring their trash home to throw it properly. This practice comes from Shinto and Buddhist beliefs that teach purity, respect, and order. Clean surroundings show respect for others and for nature. When entering someone's home, people say “Ojama shimasu”, meaning “I will be intruding.”

When riding a train or bus in Japan, people must stay quiet and respectful.
Here are common train rules:

Don't talk loudly or take phone calls.

Don't eat unless you are on a long-distance train like the Shinkansen.

Use priority seats for the elderly, pregnant women, or people with disabilities.

Don't litter or leave trash behind.
If a train is delayed, passengers can get a train delay certificate (Densha Chien Shoumeisho) to show at school or work as proof of being late.
Japanese trains are known for being clean, quiet, and on time.

The two main religions in Japan are Shintoism and Buddhism, and many people follow both.

Shintoism is Japan's native religion that worships kami (spirits) found in nature like trees, rivers, and mountains.

Buddhism came from other Asian countries and teaches mindfulness, simplicity, and letting go of desires.
Both religions influence Japanese festivals, traditions, and respect for nature. Examples include Obon (honoring ancestors) and Hanami (cherry blossom viewing).

Traditional Japanese arts show beauty, balance, and love for nature. Examples include:

Origami - folding paper into shapes like cranes or flowers.

Bonsai - growing small trees in pots to look like real ones.

Hina Dolls and Daruma Dolls - used for festivals and good luck.

Japanese Lanterns - used for decoration during festivals.

Woodblock Paintings - like “The Great Wave off Kanagawa” by Hokusai.
These arts are still used today to celebrate culture and tradition.

Japan is famous for dishes such as:

Sushi - rice with seafood or vegetables.

Sashimi - thin slices of raw fish.

Ramen - noodle soup with toppings like pork and egg.

Onigiri - rice balls wrapped in seaweed.

Bento - lunch boxes with rice, meat, and vegetables.

Takoyaki and Yakitori - common street foods.
Japan is also known for its Tea Ceremony (Chanoyu) using Matcha, a green tea powder symbolizing peace, mindfulness, and respect.

Karaoke comes from two Japanese words: “kara” (empty) and “okesutora” (orchestra), meaning “empty orchestra.”
It started in Kobe, Japan, in 1971 by musician Daisuke Inoue. People sing using a microphone with background music. It became popular around the world as a fun way to enjoy music.

Japanese people use honorifics at the end of names to show respect and social position.
Here are the common ones:

-san (さん) - polite and neutral, used like Mr. or Ms. for anyone.

-kun (くん) - used for boys, younger males, or close male friends.

-chan (ちゃん) - used for children, close friends, or when showing affection.

-sama (さま) - very respectful, used for customers or people of high status.

-sensei (先生) - used for teachers, doctors, or experts.
Using the wrong honorific can sound rude, so people choose carefully based on the relationship.

Japanese culture teaches many values that shape how people act:

Wabi-sabi - finding beauty in imperfection and simplicity.

Ikigai - reason for living or purpose in life.

Bushidō - the samurai code of honor, loyalty, and courage.

Gaman - enduring hardship with patience and dignity.

Omotenashi - showing kindness and hospitality without expecting anything in return.
These values guide daily life, manners, and relationships in Japan.

The most famous traditional clothing is the Kimono, a long robe tied with a sash called an Obi. It is worn during ceremonies, festivals, and special occasions.
Other types include the Yukata, a light summer version of the kimono, and Hakama, pants worn by samurai and martial artists. Traditional clothes represent elegance, respect, and connection to culture.

Modern Japanese culture has a big influence around the world through:

Anime - animated shows like Naruto, One Piece, and Demon Slayer.

Manga - Japanese comics read from right to left.

Video Games - companies like Nintendo and Sony created famous games like Pokémon and Super Mario.

Kawaii Culture - meaning “cute,” seen in characters like Hello Kitty and Cinnamoroll.

Idols and Cosplay - singing groups and fans dressing as characters.
These show Japan's creativity, imagination, and love for art and technology.
